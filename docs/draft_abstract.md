# Comprehensive meta-analysis of Zamzam water chemical composition: a multi-source independent assessment

**Draft — v0.1 — March 2026**

**Target journals**: Applied Water Science (Springer) | Heliyon (Elsevier)

---

## Abstract

**Background.** Zamzam water, sourced from a well located within the Masjid al-Haram complex in Mecca, Saudi Arabia, supplies over 2 million liters per day during peak Hajj season. Despite its significance to over 1.8 billion Muslims worldwide and ongoing public health interest, comprehensive independent assessments of its chemical composition remain fragmented across disparate studies with varying analytical methods and sampling protocols. Media reports of arsenic contamination (2011) further highlight the need for a systematic, evidence-based evaluation.

**Methods.** We performed a meta-analysis of 5 peer-reviewed studies published between 2009 and 2025 (Al-Gamal 2009, Shomar 2012, Donia & Mortada 2021, Bhardwaj 2023, EJEBA 2025 review), covering 23 chemical parameters analyzed by ICP-MS, ICP-OES, and ion chromatography. Inter-study variability was assessed using coefficient of variation (CV). Results were compared against WHO Guidelines for Drinking-water Quality (4th edition) and benchmarked against four European mineral waters (Evian, Vittel, Volvic, San Pellegrino). Rainfall data from the Open-Meteo Archive API (2019–2025) was used to explore potential recharge-composition correlations.

**Results.** The mean chemical profile of Zamzam water is characterized by elevated Na (175 ± 30 mg/L), K (45 ± 4 mg/L), Cl (165 ± 8 mg/L), and TDS (900 ± 110 mg/L), consistent with prolonged water-rock interaction in a crystalline basement aquifer. Inter-study CV was below 25% for all major ions (Ca, Mg, Na, K, Cl, SO4, HCO3), indicating high reproducibility across independent laboratories. All measured parameters comply with WHO health-based guidelines. Sodium marginally exceeds the WHO aesthetic threshold (200 mg/L) in one study but remains below the health-based limit. Arsenic levels measured by ICP-MS in peer-reviewed studies (0.006–0.013 µg/L) are 770–1,670 times below the WHO guideline of 10 µg/L, contradicting non-peer-reviewed media reports. Zamzam's mineral fingerprint is distinctive: Na-K-Cl dominant, differentiating it from Ca-HCO3 type waters (Evian) and Ca-SO4 type waters (Vittel).

**Conclusions.** This meta-analysis confirms that Zamzam water is safe for human consumption under WHO guidelines and exhibits a remarkably stable chemical signature across 16 years of independent analyses. The arsenic controversy is attributable to testing of uncontrolled retail samples with non-accredited methodology. We identify key research gaps including the need for isotopic dating (δ18O, δ2H, ¹⁴C), longitudinal temporal monitoring, and microbiome characterization.

**Keywords:** Zamzam water, hydrochemistry, meta-analysis, WHO guidelines, drinking water quality, arsenic, mineral water comparison
